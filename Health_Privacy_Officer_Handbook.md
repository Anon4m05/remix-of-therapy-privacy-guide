Privacy versus Confidentiality
 “Privacy” According to Wikipedia, privacy
is “the ability of an individual or group to
seclude themselves or information about
themselves,      and     thereby     express
themselves       selectively.    …    When
something is private to a person, it usually
means that something is inherently special
or sensitive to them. The domain of
privacy partially overlaps security
(confidentiality) which can include the
concepts of appropriate use, as well as
protection of information. Privacy may
also take the form of bodily integrity.”
Other sources use language to describe
privacy as:
   •   the freedom from unauthorized intrusion
   •   the state of being let alone
   •   the ability to keep certain personal matters to oneself
   •   access to one’s own personal information
Privacy relates to our fundamental right to control information about ourselves (including
the collection, use and disclosure of and access to that information). Privacy is much
broader than the concept of “confidentiality”.
“Confidentiality” is an obligation on health care providers and organizations to protect
personal information in their care, to maintain its secrecy and not misuse or wrongfully
disclose it

Personal Health Information (PHI)




Personal health information or “PHI” is a defined term in PHIPA.
It means identifying information (oral or recorded) about an individual and includes
information about (among other things):
   •   Physical or mental health (family history)
   •   Health care provider
   •   Health number
   •   Body parts or bodily substance or tests or exams
   •   Substitute Decision Maker’s name
PHI does not include employee information (except occupational health information may
be personal health information).
“identifying information” is also defined in PHIPA and means information that identifies an
individual or for which it is reasonably foreseeable in the circumstances that it could be
utilized, either alone or with other information, to identify an individual.
PHIPA also introduces a concept of “mixed records” which means that PHI includes
identifying information that is not PHI but that is contained in a record that contains PHI.

     Chapter 2: Cultivating a Culture of
                  Privacy




We are the trust builders
We are going to talk a lot about compliance in this handbook. Privacy compliance is of
course a big part of what a Privacy Officer does. But, a Privacy Officer is always in search
of what Chris Pahl, a Privacy Compliance Program Leader at Southern California Edison,
calls a “compelling why”:
        Training teaches the “what” and the “how” of privacy, but adding to those a compelling
        “why” is what begins to create culture. Beyond explaining how principles translate into
        specific requirements and obligations, it is important to help individuals understand why
        privacy matters at all.
Why is privacy important?
Because it is essential for building trust.
Trust pops up time and time again in privacy breach stories.



                                                                                                    12
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




In the 2017 sentencing hearing for a social worker in an Ontario family health team who
was successfully prosecuted for looking at the health records of five clients without
authorization (she had accessed many other clients’ health records without authorization
but was prosecuted for five), one of the victims told her story and explained the privacy
breach as a violation of trust:
      There are no words to describe how I feel today. … you betrayed our trust. Trust in a system
      to help us understand our grief, trust that our story is our own, and trust that as a mother,
      I promised my children a safe place for their emotions. … You knew my family and you
      knew we had a story. You took the privacy of four people, two of those being children, and
      you decided that your curiosity was more important, and I hope today you are held
      accountable for that.
In the reasons for the sentence of a personal fine of $25,000 to the social worker, Justice
Hampson stated:
      The various victims have provided victim impact statements which are quite telling in
      terms of the sense of violation, the loss of trust, the loss of faith in their own health care
      community, and the utter disrespect that <name removed> displayed towards these
      individuals. One individual described being vulnerable. There is a fear of misuse of the
      information, a fear for future treatment. There is a sense of betrayal, a sense of
      embarrassment, a sense of being exposed. Numbness and shock from others. Overall, the
      victim impact statements reveal a lack of trust and a sense of reluctance to share
      information with future health care providers.
      I believe this is a truly significant factor, given that we all must believe that when we go to
      the doctor for our physical illness and other mental health illnesses, that we will be able to
      trust our own health care practitioners and their team and that what we tell them will be
      respected and held in confidence so we receive the treatment and care we deserve.
A trustworthy health care system requires robust privacy practices. Privacy Officers are
trust builders.
Privacy Officers cultivate the four fundamental elements of trust in a health care
environment: competence, reliability, integrity and communication.
      Competence: The environment is safe. The team have sufficient skills and capability
      to provide the services.
      Reliability: The team does what they say they will do. The experience is consistent.
      Integrity: The team has the best interests of clients at heart. The team and client
      share the same goals and values. The team operates truthfully and honestly.
      Communication: Information is proactively available. The rules are transparent,
      understandable and accessible. Questions are welcomed and answered.

Not everyone sees the role of Privacy Officer in a positive light. Privacy Officers are
sometimes seen as humourless enforcers or wet blankets telling everyone else what they
can’t do.
Here is an inherent challenge for you:
As a Privacy Officer, how do you avoid terrifying your team into privacy paralysis or
constantly sounding like a wet blanket of what “can’t be done”?
The answer is culture. Privacy Officers must be able to identify the privacy rules and
describe the steps to take to achieve compliance. And, to avoid being ignored by team
members or viewed as a barrier to care, you must also be able to tap in and explain:
   •   WHY privacy is important
   •   WHY clients care about privacy
   •   WHY your team members should look for ways to be more privacy respectful
   •   WHY privacy is a way of demonstrating organizational commitment to your
       mission, vision and values
This handbook along with the live training will help you identify your “Compelling Why” so
you can share that enthusiasm and purpose with your team.

Indigenous, Provincial and Federal Laws
In Ontario, the Personal Health Information Protection Act, 2004 (PHIPA) and its regulation
govern the collection, use and disclosure of personal health information by health
information custodians.
Recent amendments to PHIPA relate to:
   1. Increased powers of the IPC including the ability to impose administrative penalties
   2. Regulation of de-identified information
   3. Democratizing access – making it easier for consumers to access/have records in
      electronic format
   4. Requirement to maintain an electronic audit log
   5. Interoperability requirements for digital assets to be established by Ontario Health
   6. Part V.1 – allows for the shared provincial electronic health record (EHR)
For more detailed information on the recent changes please see Appendix A to this
handbook.
Depending on your type of health care organization you may have additional laws that
apply such as the Public Hospitals Act, Long-term Care Homes Act, Mental Health Act,
(Municipal) Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act, Laboratory and Specimen
Collection Centre Licensing Act, Indigenous, First Nations, Metis, and Inuit laws and specific
health profession legislation such as the Medicine Act or Nursing Act.


Case Law and Information and Privacy Commissioner of Ontario (IPC)
The IPC’s decisions and interpretations of PHIPA as well as developing case law will change
privacy rules and standards over time, necessitating updates and upgrades to your
organization’s policies, procedures, and training.

CSA Mode Code Privacy Principles
PHIPA is based on ten privacy principles, modeled on the “Canadian Standards Association
Model Code for the Protection of Personal Information”. These principles provide a privacy
roadmap for health care organizations:
  1.    Accountability   A health information custodian is responsible for personal health information
                         under its control and must designate an individual who is accountable for the
                         custodian's compliance with PHIPA (usually a Privacy Officer)

  2.    Identifying      The purposes for which personal health information is collected must be
        Purposes         identified by the health information custodian at or before the time the
                         information is collected

  3.    Consent          The knowledge and consent of the client (or any person about whom the
                         health information custodian holds personal health information) are required
                         for the collection, use, or disclosure of personal health information, except
                         where otherwise permitted or required by law

  4.    Limiting         The collection of personal health information must be limited to that which is
        Collection       necessary for the purposes identified by the health information custodian, and
                         personal health information may only be collected by fair and lawful means

  5.    Limiting Use     Personal health information must not be used or disclosed for purposes other
        and Disclosure   than those for which the information was collected, except with the consent of
        and Retention    the client or as otherwise permitted or required by law, and personal health
                         information may be retained only as long as necessary for the fulfillment of
                         those purposes

  6.    Accuracy         Personal health information must be as accurate, complete, and up-to-date as
                         is necessary for the purposes for which it is to be used

  7.    Safeguards       Personal health information must be protected by security safeguards
                         appropriate to the sensitivity of the information

  8.    Openness         A health information custodian shall make readily available to the public

ISO 27002 Code of practice for PHI security management
The key document that guides security standards for PHI in Canada is ISO 27002. It was
developed by the International Standards Organization (ISO). The ISO recommends 11
“best practices” for the protection of confidentiality, integrity, and availability of PHI:

                              11 Best Practices for the Protection of PHI

 1       Security Policy
         Develop a written information security policy to protect PHI.

 2       Organization of Information Security
         Assign responsibility to an individual/s to maintain security and to control use of PHI including
         liaison with external authorities on security matters when required.

 3       Asset Management
         Identify someone to manage information and communication technology (ICT) equipment
         “assets”, such as computers and smart phones by using an inventory checklist or system to
         classify and track these assets as well as defining ‘Acceptable Use’ policies for employees.

 4       Human Resources Security
         Ensure security before, during, and at the end of employment for all employees, contractors,
         students, and volunteers. Make sure that individuals know about their obligations to maintain
         PHI security.

 5       Physical and Environmental Security
         Secure areas and protect the facility that houses PHI and ICT. Protect equipment from risk of
         loss or damage and have a ‘Clean Desk and Clear Screen’ policy.

 6       Communications and Operations Management
         Develop and use operational procedures and monitoring that ensure PHI, network, and
         systems security is maintained.

 7       Access Control
         Control access to PHI, networks, applications and operating systems. Ensure that staff are
         using strong passwords and keep them confidential.

 8       Information Systems Acquisition, Development and Maintenance
         Build security into information technology systems and software, do regular system
         maintenance and change control.

 9       Information Security Incident Management
         Have procedures and protocols in place for managing security incidents to report, assess,
         respond to and learn from.

 10      Business Continuity Management
         Use business continuity management to protect PHI in the event of disasters or other hazards.

 11      Compliance
         Identify legal, contractual and security policy requirements and perform regular reviews to
         make sure the rules are being followed.

Regulatory College Guidance
Regulated health professionals are required to follow College guidelines. Regulatory
colleges include obligations related to privacy and confidentiality in the standards of
practice for their members. As an employer, your organization will need to keep track of
changing privacy standards that may impact your employees.


Industry Standards
Industry associations such as the Association of Family Health Teams of Ontario (AFHTO),
the Ontario Long Term Care Association (OLTCA) or the Ontario Hospital Association
(OHA) may have privacy resources to assist members to understand and comply with
PHIPA. These resources create new standards for the sector.


Insurance
You should locate, read and follow developing privacy standards from your insurance
provider to learn how to prevent potential exposure and reduce privacy risks.


Step 2: Establish who is the “Health Information Custodian”
A health information custodian under PHIPA is the person or group ultimately responsible
for compliance with the legal requirements of the Act. For many groups answering the
question who is the health information custodian is obvious. For others, it is the million-
dollar question! A health information custodian is a defined term under PHIPA (see section
3 of PHIPA). If you are on the list – you are a custodian.
However, for many multidisciplinary clinics, you may need legal advice to determine who
acts as the custodian. You may need to create a legal document to explain the arrangement
and the IPC has been critical of groups who have not documented their PHIPA relationship.
We will discuss this analysis in the live training. You may otherwise require legal advice.


                     CAUTION: This decision has legal liability ramifications. This decision
                     also directly impacts the Privacy Officer as you need to know for
                     whom you are acting as the privacy contact person.

Step 7: Inventory of Safeguards
Create an inventory of the types of administrative, technical and physical safeguards in
place to protect the PHI your organization holds against loss, theft, unauthorized access,
use or disclosure, such as:
   •   Administrative: policies; training; confidentiality pledges; checking identification;
       security questions for phone inquiries; disclaimers; asking people to stand back
       away from desks; insurance; determine who is allowed access to an electronic
       medical record and making sure departing people are removed; conducting privacy
       impact assessments and threat risk assessments
   •   Technical: passwords; encryption; anti-virus software; screensavers; firewalls;
       back-up; audits; secure disposal; unique users; automatic log offs
   •   Physical: restricting office access; alarm systems; confidential shredding bins;
       locked doors and drawers; privacy screens; security personnel; identification
       badges; sound dampening; privacy windows; curtains; private rooms; travel bags
       with locks; playing music to dampen sound
Update the inventory every three years. See the Sample Inventory in Chapter 4 of this
handbook as a guide.

                      CAUTION: Make sure your team members know you are
                      conducting random audits. Audits should be done at least once a

Sample 2: Is there a distinction between “privacy” and “confidentiality”?

In my role as Privacy Officer I am sometimes asked to explain the difference between
“privacy” and “confidentiality”. Here is an explanation of the difference:
       "Privacy" is the fundamental right of individuals to control information about
       ourselves (including the collection, use and disclosure of and access to that
       information).
       "Confidentiality" is an obligation of organizations and clinicians to protect personal
       health information in our custody or control, to maintain its secrecy and not misuse
       or wrongfully disclose it.
Sometimes health care providers wrongly think:
   •   Since we all sign confidentiality agreements within our organization that means we can
       share information within our team however we like.
   •   So long as I do not tell anyone else about a client, I can look at any client’s record for my
       own purposes.
   •   I am entitled to know everything about my clients because I am providing them with health
       care. There should be no limits to what I know about my clients.

Because clients have privacy rights:
   •   Each person who works here and with our clients’ information should only ever view, look
       at, listen to, use or disclose identifiable client information if they have a “need to know” as
       part of their job.
   •   Clients may choose to not share everything about themselves or their health information
       or their health history with us. This is commonly called using a “lockbox” or a consent
       directive. If you would like to know more about the lockbox, click here.
   •   Merely keeping the information to yourself does not mean you have not breached a
       client’s privacy.
   •   Clients are entitled to a copy of their health records regardless of where that information
       is kept (this of course includes their chart – but may also include records such as emails or
       reports in which they are mentioned).

It is a privacy breach if a person wrongly looks at health records or talks about a client with
people who do not have a legitimate need to know as part of their positions (even if they
never further share that information). We have to report privacy breaches (to me as
Privacy Officer and to the Privacy Commissioner in certain cases) and notify affected

Sample 6: What is the circle of care? How do I share information with other health care
providers?

The Information and Privacy Commissioner of Ontario has a brochure called “Circle of
Care: Sharing Personal Health Information for Health-Care Purposes”. It explains when we
can share information about our clients with other health care providers.
The “circle of care” means that we are allowed to share identifiable health information with
other health care providers for the purposes of providing or assisting in providing health
care relying on “implied consent”. When we share within the circle of care, we do not have
to specifically ask a client for permission or have written consent.
But, if a client objects (or withdraws consent) to information being shared with an external
health care provider, we cannot rely on implied consent to share information and we must
have verbal or written permission going forward (unless an exception applies).


Who can be in the circle of care:
   •   Health care practitioners (including group practices likes family health teams and
       nurse practitioner-led clinics)
   •   Public hospitals
   •   Private hospitals
   •   Psychiatric facilities
   •   Independent health facilities
   •   Community health or mental health centres, programs or services (primary
       purpose is provision of health care)
   •   Long-term care homes
   •   Placement coordinators
   •   Retirement homes
   •   Pharmacies
   •   Laboratories
   •   Specimen collection centres
   •   Ambulance services
   •   Operators of care homes (residential tenancies)
   •   Homes for special care
   •   Community support services provider (under Home Care and Community Services
       Act, 1994)




                                                                                               66
                     Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




We can share identifiable client information with any of these groups for the purpose of
providing care or assisting in providing care. If we are sharing for another reason we need
express consent or the sharing has to be otherwise permitted or required by law. For
example, there are situations where information is shared without consent under the
Health Protection and Promotion Act.


Who is NEVER in the circle of care:
   •   Police
   •   Insurance companies
   •   Employers
   •   Family members (family members who are substitute decision-makers are not in the circle
       of care, but instead get access to information as they stand in the shoes of the client)
   •   Landlords
   •   Children’s Aid Societies
   •   Workplace Safety and Insurance Board (WSIB)
   •   Regulatory bodies
   •   Teachers and schools (however, psychologists, social workers, nurses, psychiatrists,
       speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists, physiotherapists, or audiologists
       affiliated with schools may be in the circle of care if they are providing health care)
   •   Prayer or spiritual leaders

However, not being in the “circle of care” does not mean we cannot share information with
these individuals or groups. Of course we do. We just need to have express consent to share
identifiable health information with them or (and this is important) we must otherwise be
permitted or required by law to share with them. For example, there are situations where
there is a mandatory duty to report information to the WSIB or Children’s Aid Society for
example.

Sample 9: Encryption and Mobile Devices: When We Take Personal Health Information
Off-Site

What is encryption?
Encryption of a mobile device means more than adding a password to the device.
Encryption is a functional and technical requirement that helps to ensure that client
information stored on a mobile device is inaccessible to unauthorized users by encoding
such information into a meaningless arrangement of letters, numbers and symbols.
Encryption can only be accomplished using a computer algorithm and encryption keys.


Why is Encryption Important?
We have a duty to protect client information. We are required to put into place safeguards
that secure client information against theft, loss, and unauthorized use or disclosure. As the
storage of client information on mobile devices increases so does the risk that such
information may fall into the wrong hands because of the portability, and, in some cases,
the small size of such devices, it is critical for mobile devices containing client information
to be safeguarded, including by being strongly encrypted. The failure by our organization
or a team member to ensure that a mobile device containing client information is strongly
encrypted constitutes a breach of the law that governs client information, the Personal
Health Information Protection Act, 2004 (“PHIPA”).
However, encryption alone is not enough! The Privacy Commissioner has stated that in
order for client information stored on a mobile device to be secure, additional security
measures are required, such as a strong password.
To read more about how the Privacy Commissioner has reviewed security measures, such as
encryption, see the following Orders:
   •   HO-004 – Hospital for Sick Children - a stolen unencrypted laptop (Click here)
   •   HO-007 – Regional Municipality of Durham – lost unencrypted USB key (Click here)

In each Order, the Privacy Commissioner determined that the security measures used by
the organizations in respect of mobile devices which did not include encryption, breached
PHIPA.

You may wonder why as a team member you cannot have direct access to your own health
record or to the health records of your children or elderly parents or others for whom you
are their substitute decision-maker.
   •   The information in a health record belongs to the client and the client (or their
       substitute decision-maker) has a right of access. However, that right of access is
       not unlimited and it is not “instant”.
   •   All of our clients (and their substitute decision-makers) access their records in the
       same way. And at our organization that means you can use the client portal or
       make a verbal or written request to your health care provider/health records
       department.
   •   The organization as a health information custodian also has rights and
       responsibilities about providing access to the record. The custodian has a right or
       discretion to:
           o Know that a client has asked for a copy of a record (and keep a log of those
               requests)
           o Review the health record before it is released to the client (or SDM)
           o Consult a physician or psychologist if there is a concern about safety in
               releasing the health record to the client
           o Ensure that there is nothing misfiled in the record before it is released (such
               as another client’s information or administrative documents that are not
               subject to a right of access)
           o Deny a request for access to a record under certain circumstances
           o Keep a log or a copy of health information provided to a client (or SDM)
           o Stamp the client’s copy of their health record as “Client Copy” so that if it is
               misplaced or lost it is easily identifiable that it was the client who lost the
               record (and not the custodian)
           o Take up to 30 days to respond to a request for access (and another 30 days
               if necessary)
   •   The health care provider may wish to meet with the client before communicating
       certain health information to the client (like a diagnosis or test results) in order to
       provide context, support and information about next steps – as opposed to the
       client just learning of it directly for the first time without that meeting
   •   There may be some content in the record that is subject to an exclusion from a
       right of access. For example, clients do not have a right to access:

Sample 11: What do I need to do to protect client privacy when working from home?
Here are some working from home tips so that we can do our best to avoid privacy
breaches:
   •   For electronic files, remote access to client information should be through our
       secure server (our virtual private network), where we can protect it. Every time
       client information is saved to a mobile device there is a chance it may be lost or
       stolen. Therefore, do this only when absolutely necessary to carry out your job and
       only on an encrypted mobile device.
   •   Do not use unsecured WiFi to access our secure system.
   •   Where there is no choice but to take information off-site, client information should
       be de-identified if possible. Note: merely removing someone’s name from a record
       does not necessarily anonymize the record.
   •   If client information must remain identifiable when off site (or if new client
       information must be collected and documented outside the secure server):
       •   For paper records:
              o Keep papers in a locked box or bag for transport and do not leave files in
                your car or public transit.
              o Do not store client information at home except in very limited
                circumstances. Client information at home must be held securely. Care
                should be taken to avoid family or friends or other visitors from having
                any access.
              o Do not print records containing client information at home unless
                absolutely necessary.
              o Paper records no longer needed must be either shredded with a cross-cut
                or confetti shredder on site (meaning at home or other remote work
                location) or brought back to the workplace for secure destruction. Do not
                put paper records containing personal information in the garbage or
                recycling.
       •   For electronic records:
              o Only save to a mobile device with strong encryption. Strong encryption is
                more than just password protected. If you are not sure how to encrypt a
                mobile device, ask the Privacy Officer.

Privacy Breach Checklist
When there is a privacy breach – I review the IPC’s Privacy Breach Protocol and use this
checklist …
1. Pull and implement your privacy breach protocol
Your privacy breach protocol may vary slightly in the steps; you may need to consider the
order and priority of a given step in the checklist and customize to your circumstances.
   •   Ensure appropriate team members are immediately notified of the breach.
   •   Address the priorities of containment and notification as set out in the following
       steps.
   •   Consider engaging legal counsel or a privacy breach coach if appropriate.
   •   Consider notifying the IPC. As time passes, the Privacy Officer will revisit this need
       to report on an ongoing basis. It may be premature to report if unclear whether there
       has been a breach or if unclear of the scope of the breach. The Privacy Officer may
       need to keep the IPC apprised throughout this process and may seek advice.


2. Contain the breach
Identify the scope of the potential breach and take steps to contain it.
   •   Determine whether the privacy breach would allow unauthorized access to any
       other personal health information (or other electronic information system) and take
       whatever necessary steps are appropriate (e.g. change passwords or identification
       numbers, temporarily shut down a system, suspend an individual or group’s access
       to the system, implement security, institute a restriction to the file).
   •   Retrieve and secure any personal health information that has been disclosed or
       inappropriately used or collected (including all electronic or hard copies). This might
       include attending at the scene to determine whether there are any other records in
       public.
   •   Consider whether a security specialist is required. Consider calling police.
   •   Ensure that no copies of personal health information have been made or retained by
       the individual who was not authorized to collect, use or receive the information.
       Obtain the person’s contact information in the event that follow-up is required.




                                                                                                 86
                      Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




3. Consider whether this case must be reported to the IPC and when.
As time passes, you should revisit whether to report or seek advice from the IPC. It may be
premature to report if you are unclear whether there has been a breach or you are unclear
of the scope of the breach. You may need to keep the IPC apprised as you go. Remember,
there is mandatory reporting to the IPC of certain kinds of breaches in the moment as well
as annual statistical reporting of certain kinds of breaches.


4. Investigate
Getting your arms around the details is essential. Consider whether there is sufficient
expertise to conduct an internal investigation or whether a specialist (such as a privacy or
IT security specialist) is required. An initial investigation may confirm a single incident and
that will require you to investigate further.
   •   Determine the scope of the breach:
          o Details of the incident and how it was discovered
          o Number of people affected
          o Who was involved – who else is effected
          o Dates
          o Type of incident (such as:)
                      Unauthorized use
                      Unauthorized disclosure
                      Hacking, malware, security breach
                      Lost/stolen mobile device
                      Lost/stolen hard copies
                      Refused access or correction request
                      Fax to wrong number
                      Email to wrong recipient
   •   Determine how it happened and who was involved and why
       How do you think the breach occurred? Whom will you need to interview or enlist
       to assist? Is there any technical information (e.g. related to network security, vendor
       practices and/or legal agreements)?




                                                                                                  87
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




5. Determine whom to alert of the breach
As time passes, consider updating key stakeholders. Do not forget IT providers or external
partners with whom you share IT, email or eMR/eHR systems. Also, consider whether
police need to be called. You may wish to obtain legal advice on how to proceed.


6. Determine the method of giving notice to affected individuals
If you determine there has been a breach – determine how you are going to communicate
the breach to affected individuals. Who will notify? How will the notification be done?
When?
Under section 12(2) of PHIPA, a HIC is required to notify affected individuals at the first
reasonable opportunity if their information is lost, stolen or used by or disclosed to an
unauthorized person and include in the notice a statement that the individual is entitled to
make a complaint to the IPC.
You may choose to deliver the news by phone, letter or in person or depending on the
circumstances, a notation made in the client’s file to be discussed at their next appointment.
It may depend on the person affected. Sometimes a public notice is the most efficient and
effective method of notice. Give careful consideration to whether affected individuals need
to know immediately (especially where despite your efforts, the breach is ongoing, or
where the information in question is of a highly sensitive nature or there is reason to believe
that it will be used in a malicious way). Focus on considerations such as:
   •   The potential privacy impact of calling that individual’s home or of sending a letter
   •   Whether the affected individual will be coming into the organization very soon and
       could be told in person
   •   Whether anyone on the list is deceased or is a child, or is incapable to make
       information decisions, such that notice would be given to a substitute decision-
       maker and consider the best way to manage those sensitive issues.
   •   Whether anyone on the list is otherwise “vulnerable” and consider the best way to
       manage those individuals and whether delayed notification is appropriate.
Provide details of the extent of the breach and the specifics of the personal health
information at issue.
Advise affected individuals of the steps that have been or will be taken to address the
breach, both immediate and long-term.

sample “PHIPA notification” templates (stolen computer, misdirected fax, and records

7. Establish a communications plan

receive calls about the privacy breach (e.g. a receptionist who may be fielding calls from

matter and that it is not for general discussion in the workplace or elsewhere. Share only
with individuals who absolutely need to know.
Have a plan in place to handle follow up contact from those who receive notice of the
breach, the general public who hear about the breach, and the media.


8. Further investigate and remediate
Conduct an internal investigation into the matter. The objectives of the investigation will
be to:
   •   Ensure the immediate requirements of containment and notification have been
       addressed.
   •   Review the circumstances surrounding the breach.
   •   Review the adequacy of existing policies and procedures in protecting personal
       health information.
   •   Address the situation on a systemic basis.
   •   Identify opportunities to prevent a similar breach from happening in the future.
Change practices and policies as necessary. Ensure Team Members are appropriately re-
educated and re-trained with respect to compliance with the privacy protection provisions
of PHIPA and the circumstances of the breach and the recommendations of how to avoid it
in the future.

Reporting Privacy Breaches to the IPC
PHIPA’s regulations require mandatory reporting to the IPC in the case of theft, loss or
unauthorized use or disclosure of personal health information in specific situations.
The IPC has guidelines on mandatory reporting and a form to fill out. in the following
circumstances:
1. Use or disclosure without authority: The custodian has reasonable grounds to believe
   that the personal health information was intentionally used or disclosed without
   authority. Such as snooping cases.
   Note: generally, not accidental breaches
2. Stolen Information: If information is stolen – or through something like a ransomware
   or hacking attack.
   Note: no report if the information was encrypted
3. Ongoing Risk: The custodian has reasonable grounds to believe that the personal health
   information that was used or disclosed without authority has been or will be
   subsequently used or disclosed without authority. Threat of “making it public”.
4. Pattern of similar breaches: Systemic privacy breaches (like resulting from
   automation).
5. Disciplinary action against College member or Non-College member: The custodian
   has given notice to a regulatory College in accordance with the Act for that theft, loss or
   unauthorized use or disclosure of personal health information OR would have had to
   give notice to a regulatory College if the agent was a member of a College.
6. Significant breach: The custodian determines that the theft, loss or unauthorized use
   or disclosure is significant having regard to all relevant circumstances including:
       o the nature of the personal health information that was stolen, lost or used or
         disclosed without authority;
       o the number of records of personal health information that were stolen, lost or
         used or disclosed without authority;
       o the number of individuals whose personal health information was contained in
         the record or records that were stolen, lost or used or disclosed without
         authority; and
       o the number of health information custodians or agents responsible for the theft,
         loss or unauthorized use or disclosure.

Unauthorized collection by means of the EHR
Custodians may collect, use, and disclose personal health information by means of the
electronic health record (EHR) according to the rules set out in Part V.1 of PHIPA.
In the EHR context, custodians must comply with the breach notification requirements set
out elsewhere in the act, as well as an additional requirement: if personal health
information is collected without authority by means of the EHR, the custodian responsible
for the collection must notify the IPC if the unauthorized collection by means of the EHR
had been a use or disclosure under any of the six circumstances described above, the
custodian must notify the IPC at the first reasonable opportunity.
For example, one of the circumstances described above is that the use or disclosure is part
of a pattern. This means that the custodian must notify the IPC if an unauthorized collection
by means of the EHR is part of a pattern.

Consent, Capacity and Substitute Decision-Making
Of the ten privacy principles discussed at
the beginning of this handbook, “Consent”
is the most important.
The knowledge and consent of a client are
required for the collection, use or
disclosure of that client’s PHI, except as
otherwise permitted or required by law.
In PHIPA, that principle is enshrined in
section 29:
A health information custodian cannot
collect, use or disclose personal health
information unless:
   •   The Individual consents and the
       collection, use or disclosure is
       necessary for a lawful purpose, or
   •   The collection, use or disclosure is permitted or required by PHIPA


And this consent is subject to two general limiting principles found in section 30 of PHIPA:
A health information custodian cannot collect, use or disclose personal health information:
   •   If other information will serve purpose
   •   More information than is necessary for purpose


In Alberta they call that the “Prime Directive” – don’t use health information if you don’t
need it. And only use the least amount you can.

Capacity not age
Just like consent to treatment, there is no magic age at which someone automatically starts
to make their own health information decisions. A person may make information decisions
if they are “capable”.


Capacity test
The test for capacity to make health information decisions is found in PHIPA.
First, all clients are presumed to be capable to make health information decisions, unless it
is not reasonable to presume capacity.
For example, babies are never capable of making health information decisions. Very young
children are rarely able to make their own health information decisions. But as people age
they may become more capable of making their own choices about their own health
information.
Some individuals never become capable (maybe due to an intellectual limitation or
developmental delay) and some individuals lose capacity (if they are unconscious or due to
illness or intellectual decline).
Capacity is a two-part test; to be considered capable a person must have the ability to:
       •   understand the information that is relevant to the decision to collect, use or
           disclose their health information, and
       •   appreciate the reasonably foreseeable consequences of their choices about
           the collection, use or disclosure of their health information (giving, not giving,
           withholding or withdrawing the consent).
If a person fails either part of the two-part test, the person is incapable of making health
information decisions.
Who decides if someone is capable? The health care provider who is proposing the
treatment about which the information relates or the health care provider who holds the
information decides.




                                                                                                111
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Who can make decisions about the collection, use and disclosure of health information?
This is a bit complicated. See section 23 of PHIPA.
Let’s start with a case where a person is “capable”:
       •   A capable person may make decisions about their own health information
           AND
       •   If the capable person is over the age of 16, they may authorize someone else to
           act on their behalf to consent to the collection, use or disclosure of health
           information (that person must be at least 16 years of age) (note, for example, a 17-
           year-old may write that their friend who is also 17 can authorize sending information
           to an insurance company while the client is away at school out of town)
           AND
       •   If the capable person is under the age of 16, their parent (or CAS or other person
           lawfully able to make decisions instead of a parent) may also make information
           decisions UNLESS the information relates to:
              o treatment about which the capable child made a decision on their own
                under the Health Care Consent Act OR
              o counseling about which the child participated on their own under the
                Child, Youth and Family Services Act, 2017
OR UNLESS the capable child says they do not want the parent to make that decision. A
capable child’s decision to exclude their parents from making health information decisions
trumps the permission of a parent to make some decisions.




                                                                                                   112
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Now, we’ll deal with an incapable person. If a person is found to be incapable of making
information decisions, only the person’s substitute decision maker (SDM) can make
decisions on that person’s behalf. The ranked list of who can be a substitute decision maker
is in section 26 of PHIPA. The ranked list includes:
       •   The incapable person’s guardian of the person or guardian of property, if the
           guardian has authority to give or refuse consent to health information decisions
           (note, very few people have a “guardian of the person” – this is not the same as “legal
           guardian” as used in other contexts like school notes)
       •   The incapable person’s attorney for personal care or attorney for property, if
           the consent relates to the attorney’s authority to make a decision on behalf of
           the individual (note, children under the age of 16 cannot execute a power of attorney
           for personal care –so they will not have one. Children under the age of 18 cannot
           execute a power of attorney for property – so they will not have one. A power of
           attorney for personal care and a power of attorney for property are both documents -
           if someone claims to have the authority to act as an attorney for personal care or an
           attorney for property you should ask to see a copy of the document)
       •   The incapable person’s representative appointed by the Consent and Capacity
           Board (CCB), if the representative has authority to give or refuse consent to the
           information decision (note, very few people have a personal representative
           appointed by the CCB)
       •   The incapable person’s spouse or partner
       •   A child or parent of the incapable person, or a children’s aid society or other
           person who is lawfully entitled to give or refuse consent to the information
           decision in the place of the parent. This paragraph does not include a parent who
           has only a right of access. If a children’s aid society or other person is lawfully
           entitled to give or refuse consent to the information decision in the place of the
           parent, this paragraph does not include the parent.
       •   A parent of the incapable person who has only a right of access.
       •   A brother or sister of the incapable person.
       •   Any other relative of the incapable person.
A SDM must be at least 16 years of age, unless the parent of an incapable child.
This is complicated. Let’s work through some examples:




                                                                                                     113
                   Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Example 1: A school needs information about a 14-year old’s vaccinations. Express
consent is needed for the team to release that information to the school (the school is not a
health information custodian). The team can rely on the following people to sign the form:
           1. If the 14-year old is incapable of making the decision and the parents are
              substitute decision makers for the child, the parents would be asked to sign
              the form.
           2. If the 14-year old is capable of making the decision to release the information
              to the school – the 14-year old can sign the form.
           3. If the 14-year old is capable of making the decision, but the records about the
              vaccinations have to do with when the child was a baby and very young and
              not making their own decisions about the vaccinations, the parent could also
              sign the form (UNLESS the 14-year old said, no I don’t give permission for my
              parent to do that).

Example 2: A parent asks to see a 15-year old child’s health record – or to be told about a
child’s diagnosis or why the child came to the team.
           1. If the 15-year old is incapable of making decisions about treatment and the
              parent is the SDM, the parent stands in the shoes of the child and gets access
              to all relevant information in order to make decisions about the child’s
              treatments. And the parent can have access to information for releasing to
              anyone else.
           2. If the 15-year old is capable of making the information decisions – the child
              may not want their parents to see the health record, or know a diagnosis or
              know why they came to the team. If the child made their own treatment
              decisions or counseling decisions, a parent cannot just get automatic access
              to the child’s information. It can only be shared with the child’s consent.
              Generally speaking, the team should consult with capable children about
              how they would like their parents involved in their decisions and how much
              information they would like shared with their parents.
           3. If the 15-year old is capable of making the decision, but the records relate to
              when the child was a baby or young child and not making their own health
              care, the team can release information to the parent UNLESS the 15-year old
              does not give permission for the parent to have that information. Generally
              speaking, the team should consult with capable children about how they
              would like their parents involved in their decisions and how much
              information they would like shared with their parents.

Consent FAQs
When a child is incapable, do parents have to make information decisions together? What
if they disagree?
If two parents are both substitute decision-makers for their incapable child, they make
information decisions together. If they cannot agree, then they should be encouraged to try
to agree. If they cannot agree, ultimately they may lose the right to make information
decisions and the health care practitioner would go to the Public Guardian and Trustee for
decisions on the child’s behalf.
What happens if a child’s parents are separated or divorced? Who makes information
decisions for the child?
In cases of separation or divorce there may be a separation agreement or court order
relating to decision-making and sharing information about a child. If one parent says there
is such a document the health care practitioner should be given a copy and should read it
carefully.
Remember, if the child is capable – the child makes information decisions even if there is a
court order that says one or both parents have custody or decision-making responsibility.
A capable child may say they don’t want parents involved.
If the child is incapable (and there are no higher ranking SDMs):
   •   If both parents have custody or decision-making responsibility, they both make
       information decisions
   •   If one parent has custody/decision-making responsibility and the other parent has
       only access/parenting time, the custodial/decision-making parent makes
       information decisions. An access/parenting time only parent may be a substitute
       decision-maker if the custodial/decision-making parent is not able to be the
       decision-maker.

Note though the IPC’s Decisions 107, 129 and 160, in which a custodial parent who made
an access or correction request in relation to a child’s or children’s health record was found
not to have authority under PHIPA to act as the child’s independent substitute decision-
maker in order to make the request given the other custodial parent’s objection. Where
there is reason to believe that an equally ranked substitute decision-maker disagrees with
the request, it would not be reasonable for the custodian to treat either substitute decision-
maker as having independent authority in respect of the request (Decision 107, Decision
129, Decision 160).




                                                                                                 115
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




This does not mean though that in every case, a custodian faced with a request from a
mentally incapable individual’s substitute decision-maker will be obliged to canvass the
views of all equally ranked substitute decision-makers to satisfy himself that they all agree
to the request.
Under section 71(4)(b) a custodian is entitled to rely on an assertion by a person claiming
to be the lawfully authorized substitute decision-maker for a mentally incapable individual,
unless it is not reasonable to do so in the circumstances.
Remember, if the child is capable consider:
       1. If the child is over the age of 16, the child decides how their information is shared
          with their parents;
       2. If the child is under the age of 16, the child’s custodial/decision-making parent or
          a society or other person who is authorized to act in the place of the parent, may
          be the child’s substitute decision-maker unless the information relates to
               a. Health care treatment the child decided on their own; or
               b. Counseling the child decided on their own.

In all cases, a capable child may tell us they do not want a substitute decision-maker
involved or that they wish to make a different choice than a substitute decision-maker. A
capable child’s instructions override any other person’s decision-making. We are not
required by law to consult with a capable child under the age of 16 to ask whether they
agree before acting on the consent of a substitute decision-maker parent or other
authorized person.
However, we may choose to consult with the child at our discretion. If we know of a capable
child’s instruction that differs from the substitute decision-maker’s instruction, we must
follow the capable child’s instruction.
But all that said, court documents have to be read carefully. Sometimes a court orders a
clinician or health care organization to release information to the court. Even about a
capable child. Sometimes you will need legal advice to get the answer right.
What if a non-custodial parent with access rights only makes a request for information
about their child? What do we need to know about the Children’s Law Reform Act and
Divorce Act?
It is important to note that under section 20(5) of the Children’s Law Reform Act (CLRA) and
section 16.4 of the Divorce Act (DA), a parent with entitlement to parenting time (formerly
an “access only” parent – both Acts have been amended to refer to decision-making
responsibility and parenting time rather than custody and access) has the right to make
inquiries and to be given information as to the health, education and welfare of the child. So
long as there is no conflict with PHIPA, parents with entitlement to parenting time may
receive information about their children.



                                                                                                  116
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




However, when we receive a request for information from an access or parenting time only
parent, we must review PHIPA to determine the appropriate response. The CLRA and DA
do not provide isolated rights of access to records we hold.
PHIPA establishes a hierarchical list of substitute decision-makers for making information
decisions. In that list, a custodial parent is higher ranked than an access only parent. (PHIPA
has not been amended to refer to decision-making responsibility and parenting time rather
than custody and access.)
For incapable children, custodial parents will be substitute decision-makers unless that
right has been removed from them or there is some other reason preventing them from
acting (such as they are out of country or unavailable or there is a no contact order or there
is a higher-ranked substitute decision maker).
Depending on the circumstances, if an access/parenting time only parent makes a request
for information as to the health, education or welfare of their child(ren), we should consider
the potential application of certain sections of PHIPA that permit the disclosure of personal
information when certain conditions are met.
Examples of where an access parent may receive information include:
    •   A capable child consents to the disclosure of their information to their access-only
        parent.
    •   An access parent may be a substitute decision-maker for making information
        decisions on behalf of an incapable child if there are no other higher-ranking
        substitute decision-makers available (such as the custodial parent is unavailable or
        unable to do so).
    •   Another individual who is a substitute decision-maker for an incapable child
        consents to the disclosure of personal information to the access-only parent.
    •   It is necessary to disclose information to an access-only parent in order to reduce
        or eliminate a significant risk of serious bodily harm.
    •   A court orders the disclosure of personal information to the access-only parent.
If a non-custodial parent makes a request for information about their child, we should ask
if there is a separation agreement or court order in relation to custody and access, or
decision-making responsibility and parenting time, and we should ask to see a copy of the
document.
If there is an agreement or court order, we will need to decide whether the agreement or
court order fulfils the conditions for disclosure without consent under PHIPA.




                                                                                                  117
                         Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




In making this determination, the IPC will expect us to consider, among other things:
    •    whether we are satisfied that the agreement or court order provided by the
         requester is currently valid, and
    •    whether and how the agreement or court order (and/or other statute – CLRA or DA)
         addresses the child’s personal information that is the subject of the request for
         disclosure. 1
If the child is capable, we can ask the child if they consent to the disclosure of the
information requested. If the child is not capable, we can ask the child’s substitute decision-
maker (likely the custodial parent) if they consent to the disclosure. If a capable child or an
incapable child’s substitute decision-maker is opposed to providing information to the
access/parenting time parent, we would not want to override their wishes.
However, this will be a highly fact-specific determination in each case and the IPC has
suggested that the child’s consent will be one factor to consider, but is not the only relevant
factor. 2 We should consider too that the IPC has stated “that the use of the term
”information” in [the relevant sections of the CLRA and DA] does not necessarily mean “any
and all” information, particularly in circumstances where disclosure may not be in the
children’s best interests”. 3
The IPC has concluded that applying the CLRA or DA provisions to disclose a child’s
personal information to access parents may be inconsistent with sections of those statutes
that codify “the best interests of the child” as a key guiding principle in matters concerning
custody and access. 4 In other words, if we believe that it would not be in the child’s best
interests to disclose the information to the access parent seeking it, we should not disclose
it, assuming that we have properly considered all of the relevant factors and can articulate
the reasons for our decision. 5
Ultimately, if a requester believes that a health information custodian has failed to comply
with a valid agreement or court order that requires disclosure of the information
requested, the requester may seek enforcement of the order through the courts.

Circle of Care




There is a lot of mythology about the circle of care. Many people misunderstand it.
The circle of care is about sharing information between health care providers for health
care purposes relying on implied consent.
It means express consent is not required to share information with a direct health care
provider of the client, when sharing the information for providing or assisting in providing
health care.
If you are in the circle of care you can rely on implied consent – but it has to be reasonable
in the circumstances that your client knows that you are going to share the information.
If your client objects to information (or withdraws consent) being shared with an external
health care provider, then you cannot share the information relying on implied consent.




                                                                                                 124
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




The section of PHIPA that we have to follow is:
18 (3) A consent to the disclosure of personal health information about an individual must
be express, and not implied, if,
(a) a health information custodian makes the disclosure to a person that is not a health
information custodian; or
(b) a health information custodian makes the disclosure to another health information
custodian and the disclosure is not for the purposes of providing health care or assisting
in providing health care.


Who can be in the circle of care:
   •   Health care practitioners (including group practices likes family health teams and
       nurse practitioner led clinics)
   •   Public hospitals
   •   Private hospitals
   •   Psychiatric facilities
   •   Independent health facilities
   •   Community health or mental health centres, programs or services (primary purpose
       is provision of health care)
   •   Long-term care homes
   •   Placement coordinators
   •   Retirement homes
   •   Pharmacies
   •   Laboratories
   •   Specimen collection centres
   •   Ambulance services
   •   Operators of care homes (residential tenancies)
   •   Homes for special care
   •   Community support services provider (under Home Care and Community Services Act,
       1994)




                                                                                             125
                   Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




You can share identifiable client information with any of these groups for the purpose of
providing care or assisting in providing care. If you are sharing for another reason you need
express consent or the sharing has to be otherwise permitted or required by law.


Who is NEVER in the circle of care:
   •   Police
   •   Insurance companies
   •   Employers
   •   Family members (family members who are substitute decision-makers are not in the
       circle of care, but instead get access to information as they stand in the shoes of the
       client)
   •   Landlords
   •   Children’s Aid Societies
   •   WSIB
   •   Regulatory bodies
   •   Teachers and schools (however, psychologists, social workers, nurses,
       psychiatrists, speech-language pathologists, occupational therapists,
       physiotherapists, or audiologists affiliated with schools may be in the circle of care
       if they are providing health care)
   •   Prayer or spiritual leaders


However, not being in the “circle of care” does not mean you cannot share information with
these individuals or groups. You just need to have express consent to share identifiable
health information with them or you must otherwise be permitted or required by law to
share with them. For example, there are situations where there is a mandatory duty to
report information to the WSIB or Children’s Aid Society for example.

Terminology: Access

•   Clients have a right of access to their own records of personal health information.

Legal Foundations

McInerney v. McDonald – Supreme Court of Canada

The right of access has a long and rich history. The most important case on the topic is the
case of McInerney v. McDonald which was decided in 1991 by the Supreme Court of
Canada.

A client made a request to her doctor for copies of the contents of her complete medical
file.

The doctor delivered copies of all notes, memoranda and reports she had prepared herself
but refused to produce copies of consultants' reports and records she had received from
other physicians who had previously treated the client, stating that they were the property
of those physicians and that it would be unethical for her to release them.


                                                                                               132
                   Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




The doctor suggested to her client that she contact the other physicians for release of their
records.

But the court said …

   •      In the absence of legislation, a client is entitled, upon request, to examine and
          copy all information in her medical records which the physician considered in
          administering advice or treatment, including records prepared by other doctors
          that the physician may have received.

   •      Information about oneself revealed to a doctor acting in a professional capacity
          remains, in a fundamental sense, one's own.

   •      While the doctor is the owner of the actual record, the information is held in a
          fashion somewhat akin to a trust and is to be used by the physician for the benefit
          of the client.

   •      The trust-like "beneficial interest" of the client in the information indicates that,
          as a general rule, she should have a right of access to the information and that the
          physician should have a corresponding obligation to provide it.

   •      The client's interest being in the information, it follows that the interest
          continues when that information is conveyed to another doctor who then
          becomes subject to the duty to afford the client access to that information.

   •      The client's general right of access to medical records is not absolute. The onus
          lies on the physician to justify a denial of access. Clients should have access to
          their medical records in all but a small number of circumstances.

PHIPA

Section 52(1) of PHIPA says: “Subject to this Part, an individual has a right of access to a
record of personal health information about the individual that is in the custody or under
the control of a health information custodian unless” an exception identified in the Act
applies.

Section 51 specifies certain types of information to which the access provisions of PHIPA
do not apply:

   •      Quality of care information (under QCIPA)
   •      PHI collected for quality assurance program under RHPA
   •      Raw data from standardized psychological tests or assessments
   •      PHI as prescribed


                                                                                                  133
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Section 52 identifies additional exceptions to the right of access:

   •       Legal privilege

   •       Another law prohibits the access

   •       Information was collected or created primarily in anticipation of a proceeding
           (and the proceeding has not concluded)

   •       Information was created in the course of an inspection, investigation or legal
           procedure for fraud detection or monitoring and the event has not concluded

   •       If granting access could reasonably be expected to:

            o Result in a risk of serious harm to the treatment or recovery of the individual
              or a risk of serious bodily harm to the individual or another person (and can
              consult a physician or psychologist to determine)

            o Lead to the identification of a person who was required by law to provide
              information in the record to the HIC

            o Lead to the identification of a person who provided information in the
              record to the HIC explicitly or implicitly in confidence if the HIC considers it
              appropriate in the circumstances that the identity of the person be kept
              confidential

Section 52(2) says that “an individual has a right to access that part of a record of personal
health information about the individual that can reasonably be severed from the parts to
which the individual does not have a right of access.”

Requests for access have to come in a certain way (in writing and with enough detail)
(PHIPA, ss. 53(1) and 53(2)).

Section 54(1) sets out the possible responses of a custodian who receives a request from an
individual for access to a record of personal health information. The custodian shall either:

    •   Make the record available and, at the request of the individual, provide a copy of the
        record and if reasonably practical, an explanation of any term, code or abbreviation
        used in the record;

    •   Give a written notice to the individual stating that, after a reasonable search, the
        custodian has concluded that the record does not exist, cannot be found, or is not a
        record to which the right of access applies; or

    •   If the custodian is entitled to refuse the request, give the individual written notice
        of the refusal in accordance with s. 54(1)(c) and (d).



                                                                                                 134
                   Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Response time is key:

   •      You must respond as soon as possible and no later than 30 days after receiving a
          written request (PHIPA, s. 54(2))

   •      If you need an extension – you must notify the requester for need of an extension
          (up to another 30 days) before the first 30 expires (PHIPA, s. 54(3)). You can
          extend the time if:

             •   Responding would interfere with operations because the request is
                 complicated (PHIPA, s. 54(3)(a)); or

             •   You have to consult and that will take longer than 30 days (PHIPA, s.
                 54(3)(b)).

   •      A notice of extension must include reasons and the length of the extension
          (PHIPA, s. 54(4)).

If you do not respond to an access request within 30 days or before any extension of the
time limit, and if the requester complains to the IPC because you do not respond, the IPC
may deem you to have refused the request for access and may order you to provide a

IPC Decisions

Reasonable Search

When you receive an access request for personal health information from a client, you must
conduct a reasonable search to locate responsive records. When you find records, you must
provide those records to the requester, subject to some limits.

For the majority of access requests, the issue of whether you did a “reasonable search” will
not arise. An individual will ask for their records and if you find records, you will give them
their records, and the individual will receive the information they desired.

You do not have to complete a broad search if presented at first instance with a request for
the normal or traditional health record. Keep the search simple at first. You must expand
the search if specifically asked to do so or if it is obvious from the request the individual is
looking for more than the normal or traditional health record.

Occasionally, an individual may object to the records they receive or the fact that they did
not receive records they were expecting and claim that you did not do a “reasonable
search”.

Very few clients who are dissatisfied with your response to their access request for their
records will use the words “reasonable search” to explain what they are concerned about.
Often a reasonable search complaint is buried in a broader complaint about health care or
information.

If a client says or writes any of the following, they often mean they think you did not do a
reasonable search:

   •   I believe that there should be additional records that were not provided to me in
       response to my access request.

   •   I think that there should be more notes. I saw you taking handwritten notes and

   •   References in the documents that you provided to me make me think that there are

If a client is concerned that you have not found records or provided all their records of
personal health information, you should ensure you did a reasonable search.

The IPC’s test of whether a custodian has completed a reasonable search includes:

       1. The custodian must provide sufficient evidence to show that it has made a
          reasonable effort to identify and locate responsive records.

       2. A reasonable search is one in which an experienced employee knowledgeable in
          the subject matter of the request expends a reasonable effort to locate records
          which are reasonably related to the request.

       3. Although a requester will rarely be in a position to indicate precisely which
          records the custodian has not identified, the requester must still provide a
          reasonable basis for concluding that such records exist.

For simple and uncontested access requests, a reasonable search will involve looking for
the client’s electronic or paper medical record. It may be sufficient to record that a request
for records of personal health information was made and a copy of records was provided
(or there were no records found.)

However, for complex or contested access requests where a client complains to you or to
the IPC that you did not do a reasonable search, you may be asked to provide evidence to
explain the following: (Note: Sometimes the IPC will ask for an affidavit explaining the
efforts.)

       1. An experienced employee did the search – Staff members who conduct a search
          for records of personal health information must be knowledgeable about the
          type of care provided, record keeping practices for that care, and any relevant
          records storage and filing systems. The appropriate person to do a search might
          be a release of information staff person, records manager, Privacy Officer,
          manager or other leader.
       2. A reasonable effort was expended in the search – You must record details of
          your search practices. For example: where you looked; how long it took you to
          conduct the search; how many documents or files were reviewed; who was
          contacted and the search parameters that were used. You do not have to exhaust
          all avenues, but you must demonstrate that you looked hard enough to find
          related records. You should record these details in the complaint management
          file responsive to the reasonable search complaint or in the affidavit or letter to
          be sent to the IPC.




                                                                                                 137
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




       3. The right places were looked in for all related records – The person who does
          the search should be provided with detailed information about the request,
          including any information needed to ensure a consistent understanding of the
          scope of the search and the approach to be taken when searching.
          A reasonable search for client records will start with searching your normal filing
          places for client records such as the EMR or historic paper records. However,
          depending on the request, you may need to look in additional places. Records
          may exist in a variety of formats such as: electronic records, paper, emails, voice
          messages, video recordings, photographs, text messages, Facebook messenger,
          instant messages and calendars.

          If the scope of the request requires you to do so, you must identify all staff
          members who may have responsive records and ask them to do a search. This
          might involve contacting finance staff, administrative staff, and executives.
          Anyone who works for or on behalf of the organization may need to be contacted
          to do a search for records.

          In some cases, it may also be necessary to search back-up tapes or other back-up
          storage media.

A custodian is not required to prove with absolute certainty that records do not exist.

The IPC applies a “reasonableness” standard. So long as a custodian demonstrates it made
a reasonable effort to identify and locate responsive records and either provided a copy of
those records or concluded there were no records or additional records, a custodian is not
required to go to extraordinary lengths to search for records responsive to a request.

A requester must provide a reasonable basis for why they believe records exist.

Although a requester will rarely be in a position to indicate precisely which records the
custodian has not identified, the IPC will expect a requestor to explain why they think
records exist. If you cannot locate records (or additional records), you are entitled to ask a
requestor for more information, such as:

   1. Why do you think we have records or additional records?

   2. Did you ever see the records?

   3. Did you watch someone writing or recording the information?

   4. Did someone tell you there were records?

   5. What interactions did you have with our staff that make you think we should have
      records?

You must contact the requester if you have any questions or require clarification about
their request. For complex access requests, you are obligated to help requesters
reformulate their requests to identify sufficiently the type of records they are seeking.

A request by a client for a copy of “my complete file” should be interpreted to mean their
electronic medical record.

Most requests for access to records of personal health information do not trigger an
organization-wide search. However, you must expand the search beyond the usual search
if specifically asked to do so or if it is obvious from the request that a broad search will be
necessary (i.e. a request for “all records of personal health information including emails
between staff, text messages, memos, reports, voice messages, video surveillance
recordings”). The scope of the request will determine the nature of your search.

A custodian does not always have to contact every individual named in an access request.

For most requests, when a requestor asks for records of a particular staff member, you need
to ask that staff member to search for records.

However, in rare cases where a requester has made an unreasonable request of you to
search and where you can prove you have spent significant resources searching, you may
choose to limit the people and places where you search to those you suspect have records.

Fees charged for access

Sections 54(10) and (11) of PHIPA provide custodians with the discretion to charge a fee
for providing an individual with access to their own personal health information. The
custodian must first give an estimate of the fee and the fee must not exceed the prescribed
amount or the amount of reasonable cost recovery. There is no regulation that prescribes
fees for access under PHIPA.

In a number of decisions, the IPC has concluded that “reasonable cost recovery” does not
mean “actual cost recovery,” or full recovery of the costs that the custodian incurs in
fulfilling an access request. The IPC has also concluded that the use of the word
“reasonable” suggests that the costs should not be excessive and should not create a
financial barrier to the right of access.

Access to records outside the traditional health record and the test of “records dedicated
primarily to”

Section 52(1) of PHIPA provides that “an individual has a right of access to a record of
personal health information about the individual that is in the custody or under the control
of a health information custodian unless” an exemption applies.




                                                                                                                                           142
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Section 52(3) of PHIPA states:

       Despite subsection (1), if a record is not a record dedicated primarily to personal
       health information about the individual requesting access, the individual has a right
       of access only to the portion of personal health information about the individual in
       the record that can reasonably be severed from the record for the purpose of
       providing access.

When preparing records for responding to access requests, you must analyze whether each
record is “dedicated primarily to personal health information about the individual
requesting access”. If so, the individual has a right of access to the entire record (subject to
the exceptions to the right of access) even if it incidentally contains information about
others and other matters. If not, the individual only has a right to their own personal health
information that can reasonably be severed from the rest of the record (and still subject to
the exceptions to the right of access).

The need to analyze whether a record is dedicated primarily to personal health information
about the individual requesting access arises when, for example (this is not an exhaustive
list):

   •   An individual asks for “a copy of their file” and when you search, you find their file
       but you also find their name in their sibling’s, child’s, parent’s, spouse’s or other
       family member’s records.

   •   An individual asks for their file in relation to joint or family therapy sessions.

   •   An individual asks for a copy of a specific record that references multiple clients
       (such as other family members).

   •   An authorized substitute decision-maker wants copies of emails between staff
       members about their complaint relating to their child’s health care.

   •   A client wants a copy of your report to a regulatory College about their health care
       provider’s involvement in the requester’s care that resulted in a disciplinary matter
       and termination of employment.

   •   A client wants a copy of minutes of a senior management meeting where your
       leaders discussed the lawsuit brought by the requester against your organization.

   •   An individual complains to the Information and Privacy Commissioner of Ontario
       that your analysis of whether a record is dedicated primarily to personal health
       information of the individual is incorrect.

                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




If there is a dispute between your analysis and a requester’s perspective of whether a
record is dedicated primarily to the personal health information the requester, the IPC will
be asked to adjudicate the dispute.

The IPC has explained the words “dedicated primarily” in PHIPA prompt an analysis of both
the “quantity” and “quality” of the information in the record.



Quantitative – How much information

It will be relevant to look at how much of a record relates to personal health information
about the requester in terms of the number of words and the overall volume of the record.
That is a “quantitative” analysis. If the majority of the record (that is, most of the words)
relate to personal health information about the requester, the record starts to look like it is
dedicated primarily to personal health information about the requester.

However, the IPC says “quantity” is not the only analysis to determine whether a record is
“dedicated primarily” to something and sometimes, the quantity of content of a record can
be misleading. Sometimes a record includes an attachment that is clearly a record
dedicated primarily to personal health information about the requester; however, the short
cover letter or email that forwards the attachment is not itself dedicated primarily to
personal health information about the individual. The word count alone might suggest the
record is “dedicated primarily” because of the attachment, but that conclusion might not be
appropriate if the cover letter or email on its own would not meet the test as being
dedicated primarily to personal health information about the individual.

Analysis of the risk of harm

When you receive an access request for personal health information from a client, you must
provide the individual with a copy of their records unless an exception to their right of
access applies. One reason why you might deny an individual a right of access to their own
records of personal health information is if providing them with their records could cause
them serious harm or there is a risk of serious harm to someone else.

Under section 52(1)(e)(i) of PHIPA, an access request for personal health information may
be denied if “granting the access could reasonably be expected to result in a risk of serious
harm to the treatment or recovery of the individual or a risk of serious bodily harm to the
individual or another person.”

In many access requests you receive from clients, the issue of a risk of serious harm will not
arise.

However, occasionally, you will identify a situation where there is a history or episode of
violence or an allegation of risky behaviour that leads you to question whether there is a
risk of serious harm in allowing someone to have a copy of their file or records.

Concerns about a risk of serious harm will usually come from your own staff such as health
care providers, supervisors, release of information staff, legal counsel or executives, or
from information contained in the record itself (i.e. previously recorded concerns of staff or
others). Such concerns could also come from a client’s family members or third parties with
whom you deal.

If you raise or receive concerns about safety or harm such as the following, you need to take
further steps to evaluate whether there is a risk of serious harm in granting a client access
to their own health records:

   •   I am scared that if this person knows my full name, they will find out my home
       address or my social media account and stalk or harass me at home or in my personal
       capacity.

   •   I am afraid the client will kill themselves if they know their family member said “*”.

   •   This client has a history of physical violence and has verbally threatened our staff. I
       don’t want to share the parts of the record with that person that detail our
       recordings of the threats made to staff.

   •   The record details concerns about domestic violence. What should we do to ensure
       we are not releasing information to put someone in harm’s way?



When considering an individual’s request for access to all or part(s) of their health record,
you need to analyze whether there is a risk of harm.

The IPC’s analysis of risk of serious harm includes:

   1. An individual has a right of access to their own personal health information held by
      a custodian, but that right is not unlimited. A legitimate concern about a risk of harm
      justifies overriding a client’s entitlement to the personal health information you hold
      about them.

   2. If a custodian denies access to records based on the risk of serious harm, the
      custodian must demonstrate a risk of harm that is well beyond the merely possible
      or speculative, but the custodian does not have to prove that the access to the
      records will result in harm.

   3. The term “individual” is not necessarily confined to a particular identified individual,
      and may include any member of an identifiable group or organization.

When you deny someone access to their own records because of this exception and the
requester complains to us or to the IPC, you may be asked to provide evidence to explain
the following:

   1. You received a request for access by a client for records of personal health
      information.

      PHIPA references “risk of serious bodily harm” in other contexts in addition to the
      issue of denying a requester access to their own records of personal health
      information. This exception relates to an access request specifically and concerns
      you have with granting access.

      You may have to explain to the IPC the date of the access request, the scope of the
      request, the responsive records, and the specifics of which records give rise to the
      concerns of the risk of serious harm. You may also be asked to state that you intend
      to or have already denied the requester access to the whole record or part of the
      record and explain your reasons for doing so.



   2. You believe the risk of harm is more than merely speculative.

      You have to be able to provide evidence of why you reasonably expect that granting
      the individual access to the records requested could result in a risk of serious harm
      to the individual or another individual.

      How much and what kind of evidence is needed will depend on the type of issue and
      seriousness of the consequence. For example, you may believe there is a risk of
      serious harm because:

             •   your staff have been threatened by the requester or know of a threat of
                 harm to a particular person

             •   you believe the requester is highly vulnerable and at high risk of self-harm

             •   you have evidence that the requester has a history of violence

             •   you have evidence that the requester has a history of persistent and
                 harassing behaviour towards certain individuals

   The potential harm must be serious harm and more than trivial or inconsequential.

   The harm itself may be physical or psychological. “Serious” harm would include, for

      •   loss of function or ability to work or complete activities of daily living

          •   the potential embarrassment of a staff member

          •   inconvenience

          •   initiation of complaints, litigation or other proceedings

   4. You can say who is at risk of serious harm.

       You must be able to identify a specific person, people, group or organization who is
       at risk of serious harm. That might be the requestor, the requestor’s family members,
       staff members, third party providers or the public.



   5. The serious harm relates to providing the records to the requester

       You need to be able to demonstrate that the requested records relate to the risk of
       serious harm. A requester may have a history of violence or making threats but a risk
       of serious harm may not relate to the requester gaining access to records of personal
       information held by the custodian. To be relevant to deny access to records, the risk
       must be triggered by the act of granting access to the records.



An individual staff member’s subjective fear of the requester, while relevant, may not be
enough to justify the exemption.

The IPC has held that subjective fear, while an important consideration, is not, in and of
itself sufficient to justify an exemption to access. The IPC will require additional evidence
establishing the basis of any asserted fear, such as details of specific incidents,
correspondence, etc.



A custodian is permitted to consider the risk that a requester will share the information
in the analysis of whether there is a risk of serious harm.

The risk that a requester will share the information provided to them is a relevant factor, to
be assessed with all of the other relevant factors. Absent additional evidence of potential
threat or endangerment though, the possible risk of broader dissemination, for example of
staff names, may be insufficient to deny access on the basis of risk of harm.



If the record that causes harm is not the custodian’s, the same risk of serious harm
analysis and rules apply.

The general right of access in PHIPA applies to all records of personal health information in
the custodian’s custody or control, regardless of where the information originated. The
right of access is not limited to records in the custody or control of a custodian that
were created by that custodian.

Any applicable access exceptions will still apply to the record – and can be relied upon by
the custodian to refuse access – even if the record did not originate with the custodian.



If only part of the record is the problem, a custodian must grant access to the remainder
of the record and sever or redact the problematic part, if that is possible.

If only part of the record of personal health information gives rise to the reasonable
expectation of a risk of serious harm, the custodian is required to grant access to the
remainder of the record of personal health information, if the custodian can sever or redact
the information to which the exception applies.

The individual has a right to access personal health information that can “reasonably” be
severed from the part of the record to which they do not have a right of access. Personal
health information that would comprise only disconnected or meaningless snippets would
not be considered reasonably severable.



How should a custodian document in its records to alert to the fact of a risk of harm?

Staff should document clearly their concerns about risks of harm for both current and
future requests for access to health records. Staff should consider when they document
whether there are known risks at the time for the individual or their substitute decision-
maker having access to the information being recorded. If so, those risks should be
recorded at the same time.

If refusing an access request based on a risk of serious harm, a custodian must give the
requester written notice of the refusal, but does not have to tell the requester specifically
why.

If you are refusing all or part of an access request based on the risk of serious harm, you
must give written notice to the requester that you are refusing access and that they are
entitled to complain to the IPC. However, in cases involving a risk of serious harm (and with
certain other exceptions), a custodian has discretion about how to inform the requester and
does not have to identify the reason why access is being denied. The custodian can:

   •   tell the requester that access is being denied because of a risk of serious harm (i.e.
       identify the exception that is being relied upon);

   •   indicate that one of the following exceptions applies, without specifying which one:

          o the information was collected or created primarily in anticipation of or for
            use in a legal proceeding which has not concluded, or

          o granting access could reasonably be expected to:

                      result in a risk of serious harm to any individual

If a custodian denies access to a record because of a risk of serious harm, and the
requester makes a complaint to the IPC, a custodian is able to make confidential
representations to the IPC.

If a complaint is not resolved at an early stage, the IPC may decide to conduct a formal
review. The IPC typically conducts reviews in writing, by asking the parties to submit
written representations. The representations are generally shared with the other parties
unless there is an overriding confidentiality concern. If you are concerned about the
requester knowing about the situation giving rise to the risk of serious harm, you are able
to write to the IPC that you have provided information to the IPC in confidence and you do
not authorize the IPC to share those details with the requester for reasons of risk of serious
harm.

Example of Risk of Serious Harm Accepted by IPC as Reason to Deny Access

In IPC PHIPA Decision 34, a mental health facility was allowed to deny access to personal
health information based on the patient’s treating psychiatrist’s opinion that the patient
would likely misinterpret the records and incorporate the content into delusional beliefs,
which could affect nursing staff and result in possible violence against the nursing staff who
authored the records. The facility submitted that:

       At the time of the refusal to grant the complainant access to his personal health
       information he was acutely ill. Specifically, the complainant requested copies of
       interdisciplinary notes that are drafted by health care professionals, which are
       primarily nursing staff. Should the [facility] have granted him access, the
       complainant would likely have misinterpreted the content of these notes and
       potentially incorporated the content into his delusional beliefs ultimately affecting
       nursing staff.

       Importantly, this could expose the authors of the drafted notes to violence by the
       complainant. Therefore, the [facility] maintains its decision to withhold disclosure of
       the requested records to the complainant.

provided the opinion that resulted in the denial of access at the request stage:

       The opinion was based on evidence gleaned from multiple sources namely my
       findings on repeated mental state examination, opinions of members of the
       interdisciplinary team expressed during various clinical meetings (for instance, a

The IPC found that the expert evidence of the psychiatrist demonstrated a risk of harm well

Example of Insufficient Risk of Serious Harm Where Access to Records was Ordered

In IPC PHIPA Decision 90, an individual sought a copy of his file from The Canadian Red
Cross Society in relation to home care services. The Red Cross provided a copy of the file
but redacted the names of the workers who had come to his home. The Red Cross relied on
section 52(1)(e)(i) of PHIPA, saying that to provide him with their names would put the
workers at risk of harm.

The Red Cross said he had been verbally abusive towards the workers and had expressed
prejudicial views about their intelligence and the skills of women.

The Red Cross said it owed a duty of safety to its staff and to provide their names to this
individual would cause them distress and a risk that he would contact them at home. The
individual said he was entitled to know who had provided him with health services.

The IPC ordered the Red Cross to provide the full names of the workers. The IPC
considered the test under section 52(1)(e)(i). The Red Cross was not able to prove a risk of
harm that was well beyond merely possible or speculation. The IPC followed precedents
from freedom of information cases that there must be “clear and direct evidence that the
behaviour in question is tied to the records at issue in a particular case such that a
reasonable expectation of harm is established.” In this case, the individual had not made any
direct threats to staff. On the facts, the risk of harm to staff was merely speculative.

Parent Requests for Child’s Information

Chapter 6: Consent explains PHIPA’s rules regarding consent, capacity and substitute

children and parents.

Only parents with custodial/decision-making responsibility can exercise an incapable

only parents/parents with parenting time do not have a right of access under PHIPA. And,
even with children under 16, a custodial parent cannot have access if the capable child says
they do not want the parent to have access or if the information relates to treatment about
which the capable child made their own decision under the Health Care Consent Act or
counselling in which the child participated on their own under the Child, Youth and Family
Services Act, 2017.

In addition, a joint custodial parent does not have an independent right of access to their
child’s personal health information over the objection of the other parent (Decision 129,
Decision 160), though the discretionary disclosure provisions under PHIPA may allow the
custodian to disclose information to that parent.

The IPC has said that if an access-only/parenting time parent makes a request for
information, a custodian should consider the request as one for disclosure and assess
whether the Children’s Law Reform Act or Divorce Act may give the parent entitlements to
information or whether there is consent for the disclosure (Decision 96).

The IPC has upheld the best interests of the child and belief that disclosure would likely
result in a serious risk of harm to the child as reasons not to disclose to a non-custodial
parent (Decision 183).

Correction
If a health information custodian has granted an individual access to a record of his or her
personal health information and if the individual believes that the record is inaccurate or
incomplete for the purposes for which the custodian has collected, uses or has used the
information, the individual may request in writing that the custodian correct the record.

Custodians must respond to those requests within 30 days.

The clinician author of a record will consider whether to approve any change to record
before changes are made. Author may refuse to make a correction if record is professional
opinion made in good faith.

You must correct if the individual demonstrates to your satisfaction that the record is
incomplete or inaccurate for the purposes for which you use the information and if the
individual gives you sufficient information to correct the record.

If a correction is not made, clients have the right to submit a notice of disagreement to their
health record and have a right to an appeal before the IPC.

      Chapter 8: Activities that Do Not
             Require Consent




We have already identified consent as the most important health privacy principle.
However, it is important to understand that many activities involving personal health
information do not require client consent.

Why do we have the “no consent” context? Because there are two umbrella categories that
can trump the right to privacy:

   1. Safety and protection; and

   2. Functioning of the health care system

Broadly speaking, PHIPA allows us to exchange information without consent for these two
reasons.

Safety and protection trumps privacy when disclosure of personal health information is
necessary to eliminate or reduce a significant risk of serious bodily harm to a person or
group (PHIPA, s. 40). Usually this means contacting the police or an intended victim to share
information about a significant risk of serious bodily harm. If you have time to do so, you
should try to get legal advice in these types of situations.




                                                                                                161
                   Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




The functioning of the health care system trumps privacy in a number of circumstances
that come up routinely. Our health care system depends upon the availability of data to
assess patient outcomes and determine future funding. The functioning of the health care
system also requires the ability to carry out quality improvement activities – it matters to
the health care system that retroactive chart reviews and other quality improvement
exercises can be done with no consent.

In this chapter, we answer the question “What are the health care system-related
circumstances that allow us to collect, use or disclose personal health information on a no
consent basis?” In other words, what can we do with personal health information without
having to seek client consent?



Permitted or Required by Law
If you read privacy policies or organizational privacy posters you will come upon the phrase
“or otherwise as permitted or required by law”. These are important words that open up an
entire world to your organization!

If you are permitted or required by law, you can do certain activities without consent.

“Permitted by law” means that PHIPA or another law says that you MAY collect, use or
disclose personal health information without the consent of the client.

   •   There is permitted collection of personal health information under s. 36 of PHIPA

   •   There are permitted uses of personal health information under section 37 of PHIPA

   •   There are permitted disclosures of personal health information under sections 38-
       50 of PHIPA

The details or “the weeds” are explained later in this Chapter.

“Required by law” means that sometimes a law or THE LAW says you MUST collect, use or
disclose personal health information. When you are required by law to act – you do not get
the client’s permission.

The question you should always be asking about your activities with personal health
information:

Do we require/have consent or are we “permitted or required by law” to collect, use or
disclose?

You have to be clear on whether you need consent or not. And if you don’t need consent,
you still have to tell people what you are doing with their information (i.e. give notice).



                                                                                               162
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Consent versus Notice
There is a big difference between consent and notice.

   •   Consent means that clients get to choose what happens with their information

   •   Notice means you have to tell your clients what you are doing with their information
       (whether they can choose what happens or not)

You must give notice of no consent activities that you carry out with personal health
information, but giving notice does not mean that you need to ask your clients whether you
can do those activities. It means that you can proceed on a no consent basis because you
have told people what you are doing.

Your public notice needs to explain client privacy rights, how to contact you about privacy,
and how to contact the IPC. You need to make sure that every activity that you do – no
matter what the activity is – if you are using or disclosing personal health information, it is
included in your poster and/or on you website statement.

What do you actually have to do to “give notice”? Practically it means:

     Posting a statement on your website

     Writing it on a poster in your physical space

     Including a description in your client orientation materials

     Having a brochure available on request

The IPC has free posters and brochures that you can use or you can make your own.

Caution!

   •   Be careful about asking for consent when you don’t need to. You may be giving
       clients a choice to opt-out of activities that you would otherwise be allowed to
       engage in without their permission. If the client says no, and then you say that you
       actually don’t need their consent – you are potentially undermining the therapeutic
       relationship by having said that the client has a choice when they actually don’t.

   •   Be careful if you are permitted to do something without consent by law, but your
       policies and/or privacy notices or posters say you will always get consent – you may
       be putting yourself in a situation where you are asking for permission when you
       don’t need to.

   •   You don’t want to scare your clients so that they don’t come to see you or are not
       forthcoming with their information

The Weeds

PHIPA allows permitted and required collection, use and disclosure without consent. Here
are the details …



Permitted or Required COLLECTIONS without Consent
Prior to 2004 in health care, many clinicians operated on the premise “I can listen, but I can’t
tell”. They would listed to anything anyone told them about their clients, but they wouldn’t
tell a family member or someone else client information unless they had consent or were
permitted or required by law.

But that changed in 2004 because PHIPA inserted rules about collecting information. You
can collect information directly from the client, either with or without consent. Or you can
collect indirectly, from someone other than the client, either with or without consent.

                        Collect PHI from client = “direct collection”

                    Collect PHI from anyone else = “indirect collection”

The collection of information without consent is an important concept that almost never
gets discussed. This is practically how LOTS of information is collected. When you collect
without consent, it is usually from someone other than the client. It usually involves
receiving information from families, friends, or caregivers. Sometimes it might be collection
from an incapable person who cannot give consent.

                   Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




What are you allowed to collect from other people without the consent of the client?

Section 36 of PHIPA permits seven collections of PHI without consent:

   1. The information to be collected is reasonably necessary for providing health care (or
      assisting) AND it is not reasonably possible to collect directly from the individual

          a. personal health information that can be reasonably relied on as accurate and
             complete OR

          b. personal health information in a timely manner. [This is what most clinicians
             rely on when they listen to family members.]

      BUT section 37(1)(a) says – you can’t use this information you collected indirectly if
      the client expressly instructs you otherwise. So if you collect PHI with no consent,
      the client can later say, “I do not give you permission to continue to use this
      information.” [This is what causes problems if a client says “I don’t want you
      collecting information from anyone” – or “don’t use it”.]

   2. A FIPPA or MFIPPA institution is collecting information related to:

          a. An investigation of a breach of agreement or violation of law;

          b. A proceeding; or

          c. Statutory function.

   3. You are collecting from a non-HIC to carry out research (subject to REB rules).

   4. You are a prescribed entity (Cancer Care Ontario; Canadian Institute for Health
      Information; Institute for Clinical Evaluative Sciences; Pediatric Oncology Group of
      Ontario) collecting from non-HICs to carry out your purposes.

   5. The Information and Privacy Commissioner says you can.

   6. You are collecting from someone who is permitted or required by law to disclose the
      information to you.

   7. The collection is permitted or required by law generally.

and not allowed as a quality improvement activity. That question is impossible for me as an
outsider to answer. Quality improvement is vital to safe, efficient health care delivery. Your
organization must engage in quality exercises. But, what quality improvement looks like
and who is allowed to do it depends on your team environment and rules.

Quality Audit and Education Use Policies can help establish the rules and can help you
determine whether a viewing of records followed the rules.

In Decision 168 a hospital’s Education Use policy allowed patients to withdraw consent for
the use of their PHI for educational purposes. The IPC found that the hospital breached the
law by not following its own information management practices – even though it chose a
higher standard than the law required. The law requires that hospitals follow their own
privacy policies. The hospital failed to teach its staff about changes to its Education Use
policy; as a result, staff continued to read and access the patient’s health information even
after she instructed them not to do so. That breached PHIPA because the hospital failed to
instruct its agents about its own privacy practices.

Six key messages from Decision 168:

   1. Snooping: Hospitals need to train their staff that when a colleague is admitted, they
      should be thoughtful about secondary uses of their information. Repeatedly going
      back to a colleague’s health records under the guise of “I’m learning” undermines
      trust.

   2. Educational purposes are not part of the “circle of care”: “Circle of care” is sharing
      of information between health care providers to provide health care. Education is a
      secondary purpose – it is not providing health care. You cannot rely on a patient’s
      implied consent to share their information between team members for educational
      purposes.

   3. No consent needed: Hospitals and other health information custodians do not need
      patient consent to share information between team members for educational
      purposes – teaching agents. PHIPA permits that activity on a no consent basis.
      Custodians can choose to obtain consent, but there is no legal obligation to do so.

   4. Higher standard: If you voluntarily adopt a higher standard than the law requires,
      you had better be able to meet that higher standard. Do not offer patients choices
      you are not willing to respect.

  5. Train: If you implement a new policy, you have to train everyone on the new
     policy. It is the law that custodians must train all agents on their privacy policies.

  6. Settlements: When you are in a settlement situation – if you change your privacy
     rules to respect a particular patient’s needs – you need to communicate any change
     immediately to the people who may come in contact with that patient so that your
     promise is actioned right away. The hospital waited too long to explain the policy
     change to the exact people to whom it was intended to apply.

Relationship 4: Sharing with Another Health Information Custodian
There is a lot of confusion about when an agreement is needed to share health
information with other health information custodians. Here are some considerations for
Privacy Officers:



“Circle of Care”
You do not need to have a data sharing agreement or PHIPA agreement to share
information with other health information custodians for health care purposes in the
regular every day continuity of care model. You are relying on either implied or express
consent.
You may use a consent form or have a team member document the consent in the health
record. Objections could become “lockboxes” or “consent directives” which are also
documented.


When is a Written Agreement Advised?

It is recommended you put your relationship in writing with another custodian if:

   •   There will be high volume and ongoing sharing of PHI between you
   •   There will be eMR/eHR access for viewing and especially if there will be
       documentation in another’s eMR/eHR
   •   More than two custodians will be sharing
   •   The sharing of PHI is more than just for continuity of care, but involves sharing
       resources (like a quality information decision support specialist)
   •   There will be shared staff
   •   A satellite clinic will be established on another custodian’s property
   •   If research is an aspect of the relationship


EMR/EHR Access
If you are going to allow another health care organization to access your electronic health
record (or you theirs), you will likely need an electronic medical/health record access
agreement and each user needs to sign off on terms and conditions for the system.
   •   Establish health information custodian status
   •   Establish the rules of access (user terms and conditions)
   •   Control access (who is entitled to use)
   •   Give and revoke access through user names



                                                                                              196
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




   •    Establish policies about appropriate use
   •    Confidentiality commitment in accordance with PHIPA and eMR/eHR policies
   •    Read-only or able to add to chart?
   •    Who decides on client access and correction requests?
   •    Consider lockbox requests
   •    Risk allocation about access
           o Is PHI accessed “as is”, using own professional judgment?
           o Or does the eMR/eHR owner take reasonable care to ensure PHI is
                accurate, up-to-date
   •    Report privacy breach/incident
   •    Indemnity for failure to comply
   •    Insurance requirements


Satellite Clinics
If you are going to allow another health care organization to come on site to provide their

collaboration agreement) to clarify:
    •   Health information custodian status
    •   Information sharing (or not) between teams
    •   Client notification of relationship and services
    •   Access, correction and lockbox rights for clients
    •   Who will give access to the clients to their information?
    •   The terms of confidentiality for the team members who will be on site

Audits




What is a privacy audit?
It is an investigation of the electronic health record systems to detect and deter
unauthorized collection, use or disclosure of personal health information.
This is not about other privacy compliance audits that test all your team’s privacy and
information management practices.


Why audit?
Audits are an essential technical safeguard to protect personal health information. They can be
used to deter and detect collections, uses and disclosures of personal health information that
contravene PHIPA. In this way, they help to maintain the integrity and confidentiality of personal
health information stored in electronic information systems.
                                            Commissioner Beamish, IPC Order H0-013, 2014



                                                                                                     209
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




Reasons to do a privacy audit:
     Ensure patient instructions are respected
     “Good privacy practices” – a mature privacy program
     Comply with expectations of the IPC
     Reduce overall financial liability for snooping (acting as a diligent organization)
     Respond to requests and complaints
     Defend your team with evidence of good practices
     Deterrence
     Detect problems – find discrepancies
     Improve practice
     Because you said you would in an agreement (Connecting Ontario or OLIS or OHT
      or other agency)
     Forensic evidence during investigations of suspected and known security incidents


What happens if you don’t audit?
If you are being investigated by the IPC or if you are sued by someone you will be judged as
to whether you have taken “reasonable” steps to detect and deter unauthorized access to
records. This requirement to take reasonable steps comes from section 12 of PHIPA:
       Security
       12(1) A health information custodian shall take steps that are reasonable in the
       circumstances to ensure that personal health information in the custodian’s custody
       or control is protected against theft, loss and unauthorized use or disclosure and to
       ensure that the records containing the information are protected against
       unauthorized copying, modification or disposal. [emphasis added]


If you are found not to have taken reasonable steps to protect personal health information,
you are at risk of the following:
•   An order of the IPC (reputational impact)
•   Litigation (including class action)
       o Vicarious liability



                                                                                               210
                    Health Privacy Officer Handbook and Community 2023




       o Negligence
       o Contract breach
•   A prosecution under PHIPA (up to $1 million for a business, up to $200,000 for an
    individual – no corporate fines issued to date)


What are the audit rules?
PHIPA does not currently mention audits per se other than the general rules to take
reasonable steps to protect PHI (section 12).
So – what is “reasonable”? Look to IPC guidance and decisions.


IPC’s General Rules – 2015
    1. All your electronic information systems must have technical capacity to log
       activities.
    2. You must ensure that all access to PHI in electronic information systems is audited
       and monitored on an ongoing, targeted (reactive) and random (proactive) basis.
    3. You must make your agents aware that you do these activities.
    4. You should have a policy that anytime a consent directive is overridden – there is a
       notice to the Privacy Officer.
    5. You should tell your agents about the detection of previous instances of
       unauthorized access by other agents and the discipline that was applied.
    6. You should have comprehensive policy and procedure for logging, auditing and
       monitoring all electronic information systems containing personal health
       information.


IPC (2015) says logs should include:
     The individual to whom the PHI relates
     The type of PHI that was collected, used or disclosed
     The agent who collected, used or disclosed the PHI
     The date, time and location of the collection, use or disclosure
     Any override of a consent directive
     In a shared system, the identity of the custodian who provided the information to
      the shared system and the custodian on whose behalf the PHI was collected, used or
